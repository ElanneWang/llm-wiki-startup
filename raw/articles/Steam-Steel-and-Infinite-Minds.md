# Steam, Steel, and Infinite Minds

**Author**: Ivan Zhao (Co-founder & CEO, Notion)  
**Published**: December 23, 2025  
**Source**: https://www.notion.com/blog/steam-steel-and-infinite-minds-ai

## 核心论点

Every era is shaped by its miracle material. Steel forged the Gilded Age. Semiconductors switched on the Digital Age. Now AI has arrived as infinite minds.

---

## Individuals: from bicycles to cars

### The Programmer Analogy
- Simon (co-founder) is a 10× programmer, but now orchestrates 3-4 AI coding agents simultaneously
- Together they make him a 30-40× engineer
- He queues tasks before lunch or bed, letting them work while he's away
- He's become a manager of infinite minds

### Steve Jobs' Bicycle for the Mind
- In the 1980s, Steve Jobs called personal computers "bicycles for the mind"
- A decade later, we paved the "information superhighway" (the internet)
- But today, most knowledge work is still human-powered — like pedaling bicycles on the autobahn
- With AI agents, someone like Simon has graduated from riding a bicycle to driving a car

### Two Problems to Solve for General Knowledge Work

**1. Context Fragmentation**
- For coding: tools and context live in one place (IDE, repo, terminal)
- General knowledge work is scattered across dozens of tools
- Example: drafting a product brief needs Slack threads, strategy docs, metrics dashboards, institutional memory
- Today, humans are the glue, stitching all that together with copy-paste and browser tabs
- Until context is consolidated, agents will stay stuck in narrow use-cases

**2. Verifiability**
- Code has a magical property: you can verify it with tests and errors
- Model makers use this for reinforcement learning to train AI to code better
- But how do you verify if a project is managed well, or if a strategy memo is any good?
- We haven't yet found ways to improve models for general knowledge work
- Humans still need to be in the loop to supervise, guide, and show what good looks like

### Human-in-the-Loop Problem
- Programming agents taught us that "human-in-the-loop" isn't always desirable
- Like having someone inspect every bolt on a factory line, or walk in front of a car (Red Flag Act of 1865)
- We want humans to supervise the loops from a leveraged point, not be in them
- Once context is consolidated and work is verifiable, billions of workers will go from pedaling to driving, and then from driving to self-driving

## Organizations: steel and steam

### The Org Chart Problem
- Companies degrade as they scale and reach their limit
- A few hundred years ago, most companies were workshops of a dozen people
- Now we have multinationals with hundreds of thousands
- Communication infrastructure (human brains connected by meetings and messages) buckles under exponential load
- We try to solve this with hierarchy, process, and documentation
- But we've been solving an industrial-scale problem with human-scale tools, like building a skyscraper with wood

### Metaphor 1: Steel
- Before steel, buildings were limited to 6-7 floors
- Iron was strong but brittle and heavy; add more floors, structure collapsed under its own weight
- Steel changed everything: strong yet malleable, lighter frames, thinner walls, buildings could rise dozens of stories
- **AI is steel for organizations**: maintains context across workflows, surfaces decisions when needed without the noise
- Human communication no longer has to be the load-bearing wall
- The weekly two-hour alignment meeting becomes a five-minute async review
- The executive decision that required three levels of approval might soon happen in minutes
- Companies can scale without the degradation we've accepted as inevitable

### Metaphor 2: Steam Engine
- Early textile factories sat next to rivers, powered by waterwheels
- When steam engines arrived, factory owners initially just swapped waterwheels for steam engines, kept everything else the same
- Productivity gains were modest
- Real breakthrough: decouple from water entirely, build larger mills closer to workers/ports/materials, redesign factories around steam engines
- Later with electricity: decentralized away from central power shaft, placed smaller engines around the factory
- Productivity exploded, Second Industrial Revolution took off
- **We're still in the "swap out the waterwheel" phase**: AI chatbots bolted onto existing tools
- We haven't reimagined what organizations look like when old constraints dissolve

### Notion's Experiment
- Notion has ~1,000 employees and 700+ agents handling repetitive work
- Agents take meeting notes, answer questions, synthesize tribal knowledge
- Field IT requests, log customer feedback
- Help new hires onboard with employee benefits
- Write weekly status reports so people don't have to copy-paste
- "This is just baby steps. The real gains are limited only by our imagination and inertia."

## Economies: from Florence to megacities

### Cities Before Steel and Steam
- Until a few hundred years ago, cities were human-scaled
- You could walk across Florence in forty minutes
- Rhythm of life set by how far a person could walk, how loud a voice could carry

### The Megacity Transformation
- Steel frames made skyscrapers possible
- Steam engines powered railways connecting city centers to hinterlands
- Elevators, subways, highways followed
- Cities exploded in scale and density: Tokyo, Chongqing, Dallas
- These aren't just bigger versions of Florence — they're different ways of living
- Megacities are disorienting, anonymous, harder to navigate
- That illegibility is the price of scale
- But they offer more opportunity, more freedom, more combinations than a human-scaled Renaissance city could support

### Knowledge Economy Transformation
- Today, knowledge work represents nearly half of America's GDP
- Most still operates at human scale: teams of dozens, workflows paced by meetings and email, organizations that buckle past a few hundred people
- We've built Florences with stone and wood
- When AI agents come online at scale, we'll be building Tokyos
- Organizations spanning thousands of agents and humans
- Workflows running continuously, across time zones, without waiting for someone to wake up
- Decisions synthesized with just the right amount of human in the loop
- It will feel different: faster, more leveraged, but also more disorienting at first
- The rhythms of weekly meetings, quarterly planning, annual reviews may stop making sense
- New rhythms emerge. We lose some legibility. We gain scale and speed.
